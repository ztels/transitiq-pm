01 ‚Äî Problem Space

A system designed for people it never considered.

Public transit apps like Google Maps and Transit App are optimized for the average commuter ‚Äî someone with a smartphone, reliable data, and no mobility challenges. But 47 million Americans have a disability, and nearly all of them rely on public transit more than the general population.

The information gap isn't about willingness ‚Äî it's about product gaps. Real-time elevator status, accessible entrance locations, and live crowding data exist in municipal APIs. Nobody has built the layer that makes them usable.

I framed this as a product opportunity: a $2.1B underserved market segment, zero dominant player, and a clear monetization path through municipal B2B contracts.

  • P1
    Elevator outage blindness 48% of wheelchair users reported missing a medical appointment in the last year due to unexpected elevator outages ‚Äî none of their apps warned them in advance.
  • P2
    Crowding anxiety for sensory needs Users with autism spectrum conditions can't pre-assess crowding levels before boarding. Existing apps offer no sensory load data, forcing avoidance of transit entirely.
  • P3
    Language-siloed accessibility resources 82% of transit accessibility info is English-only. Immigrant seniors ‚Äî a rapidly growing ridership segment ‚Äî have near-zero access to accommodation services.
  • P4
    No real-time community reporting Municipal agencies update accessibility data on monthly cycles. A broken ramp reported Monday won't be reflected in any app until 30+ days later.
Core Insight

"This isn't an accessibility problem. It's an information architecture problem ‚Äî and information architecture is a product problem."

07 ‚Äî What I Learned

The uncomfortable truths that made the product better.

01
Users lie about frequency, not pain
In interviews, users consistently underreported how often barriers affect them. But diary studies revealed 3‚Äì4 friction events per week. Triangulate behavioral data against self-reported data ‚Äî always.
02
The stigma of "accessibility" is itself a design problem
Labeling features as "accessibility" in early prototypes caused drop-off. Renaming to "Smart Trip Planning" with accessibility as an invisible layer increased adoption intent by 34% in concept testing.
03
B2B and B2C create different incentive problems
Municipal clients want compliance dashboards and liability protection. Individual users want trust and reliability. The product had to serve both simultaneously ‚Äî requiring explicit interface bifurcation rather than one unified UX.
